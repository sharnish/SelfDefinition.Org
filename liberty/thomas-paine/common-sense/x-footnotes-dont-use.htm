2  The English Constitution was not one single document, like the US Constitution, for example. 14  On April 19, 1775 British forces stationed in Boston set out to confiscate a cache of arms that they had learned were being kept by the Massachusetts militia in Concoid, a town just outside of Boston. In what is often considered the first battle of the American Revolution, even though it took place 15 months before the Declaration of Independence was signed, the Massachusetts forces ("the Minutemen") repeatedly set ambushes and sniped at the British. By the end of the day the heavily outnumbered Americans had killed 273 British soldiers and lost only 95 of their own.This battle has become the source of several of the slogans and myths of the Revolution, including "the shot heard round the world" and Paul Revere's midnight ride. Later in the text Paine calls it the "Massacre at Lexington."

15  In the eighteenth century the Jesuits were popularly perceived by Protestant English and North Americans as deceitful and conniving. Anti-Catholic feeling was very strong in the Protestant Anglo-American world, so Paine and his contemporaries often used references to the Roman Catholic church pejoratively. Another instance of this anti-Catholic bias is the frequent use of the terms derived from the Pope, popish and popery, as a synonym for tyranny or to indicate an excessive reliance on meaningless ceremony and/or mystery.

16  In his study of eighteenth-century immigration to the colonies, Hopeful Journeys: German Immigration, Settlement, and Political Culture in Colonial America, 1717-1775 (Pennsylvania UP, 1996), Aaron Spencer Fogleman notes, "By 1776, large numbers of Germans, Scots, Irish, and other 'strangers' populated the colonies, in addition to hundreds of thousands of Africans and African-Americans. The ethnic English population had become a minority" (3). In a colony like Pennsylvania, populated heavily by German immigrants (the misnamed Pennsylvania Dutch), the ethnic English population would have been even smaller. In the 1790 census, for example, 25.8 per cent of Pennsylvanians were ethnic English (Fogleman, 82). Paine's perspective was shaped by his residence in Philadelphia, so his perception more accurately reflected the ethnic diversity in his home colony than it did the situation in New England, which, due to migration patterns and other social and religious factors, tended to be more English.

18  The Stamp Act (1765) was the first attempt by the British Parliament to tax the American colonies directly. Great Britain had expended a great deal of capital defending its American colonies in the Seven Years War with France (also known as the French and Indian War) and Parliament decided that the colonies should help defray the costs of their defense. The colonists were outraged.They saw it as an unlawful tax and a violation of their rights as Englishmen: hence the famous slogan "No taxation without representation." Parliament repealed the act in 1766 but reasserted its power to tax the colonies in the Declaratory Act. A year later, Parliament ratified the Townshend Acts which included a series of taxes on glass, paper, paint, lead, and tea. The crises over the Stamp Act and the Townshend Duties are often seen as a critical prelude to the Revolution. Paine repeatedly implies that the crisis in British-American relations in 1776 is simply an extension of the same problems that had arisen with the Stamp Act.
